Chapter Overview & Clinical Significance

Chapter 17 covers infections of the cardiovascular (circulatory) system and the central nervous system (CNS) — two body systems essential for survival, where pathogenic compromise can be rapidly fatal. The chapter also addresses contamination of donated blood products and the blood-brain barrier.

⚠️ Clinical Significance — Bloodborne Pathogens & Personal Protection
  • Bloodborne pathogens are very familiar to surgical teams — extensive training on sharps injury prevention and PPE should focus on actual risks from unknown/unrecognized microbial pathogens, not just technique
  • The surgical team must apply the SAME vigilance to their own health as they do to protecting the patient
  • Most patients are unaware if they carry infectious microbes in their blood — this is why Standard Precautions presume EVERY individual is potentially infectious
  • Prevention tools: hands-free, neutral-zone, no-touch techniques for passing sharps; blunt suture needles for large/deep wound closure; eye protection for EVERY case (not just "big" cases) to prevent blood transfer to conjunctival membranes

Chapter Objectives

  • Discuss the mechanisms of contamination of blood products
  • Identify pathogens associated with infections of the cardiovascular system
  • Identify pathogens associated with infections of the CNS
  • Describe various infections caused by pathogenic invasion of cardiovascular and CNS structures
  • Apply critical thinking relating chapter material to the surgical environment of care or broader global community

Why These Two Systems Are Linked

The cardiovascular and CNS are anatomically and physiologically connected: blood circulates THROUGH the brain, and the blood-brain barrier is the key checkpoint between them. Pathogens that enter the bloodstream (bacteremia, septicemia) can travel to and infect the CNS (meningitis, encephalitis, brain abscess) — and conversely, CNS infections can have cardiovascular origins (e.g., infective endocarditis with septic emboli to the brain).

Hematology, Serology & Blood Product Contamination

Hematology — Basics
  • Hematology = the study of blood and blood-forming tissues; primarily focuses on circulatory system cells
  • White blood cells (leukocytes) — body's defense system against bacteria/viruses
  • Red blood cells (erythrocytes) — transport oxygen and carbon dioxide
  • Platelets (thrombocytes) — help prevent blood loss during injury
  • All suspended in plasma; centrifugation separates cells from plasma
  • Total blood volume: average adult has 4–8 liters (1 liter = 1.06 quarts)
  • Blood composition: cells = 45% of blood volume; plasma = 55%. Plasma = 90% water + 10% solids (proteins, carbohydrates, enzymes, vitamins, lipids, salts)
  • Whole blood = cellular components + plasma. Sera = clear, straw-colored liquid remaining after plasma is separated from fibrinogen AND blood cells have clotted

Serology & Laboratory Testing

  • Historically, serology referred to in vitro immune sera reactions: precipitin, agglutination, complement fixation
  • Currently: determines if a blood sample contains antibodies or antigens to infectious diseases by calculating serum titers
  • Lab tests: slide agglutination and ELISA
  • Western blot test — identifies bacteria in serum, including Borrelia burgdorferi (Lyme disease); also used to identify HIV
  • Western blot process: proteins separated by electrophoresis → transferred to filter (blotting) → dye-tagged antibodies washed over filter → colored band = positive test (antibody-antigen bonding)

Transmission of Infections from Donated Blood Products

Blood transfusions are usually safe and life-saving, but despite strict donor screening, blood samples can occasionally be contaminated. Infective agents transmitted through transfusion can cause serious systemic sepsis.

Bacterial

Gram-positive Staphylococcus aureus and S. epidermidis — percutaneous skin puncture by hollow-bore hypodermic needle provides entry

Parasitic

  • Tick-borne: anaplasmosis (Anaplasma phagocytophilum), babesiosis (Babesia microti)
  • Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi) — triatomine ("kissing bugs")
  • Leishmaniasis (Leishmania) — sand flies
  • Malaria (Plasmodium falciparum) — mosquito-borne

Viral

Hepatitis B (HBV), Hepatitis C (HCV), HIV, HTLV (human T-lymphotropic virus), West Nile virus (WNV), Zika virus

Prions

Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) or rapidly progressing variant CJD (vCJD)

Bacteremia, Septicemia & Infective Endocarditis

Bacteremia & Septicemia
  • Bacteremia — bacterial infection of the bloodstream. Entry: minor skin injuries, major trauma/surgery, dissemination from other body system infections, or direct access via blood transfusions
  • HAIs from surgical procedures or indwelling catheters can introduce microbes directly or indirectly (infected wound, prosthetic device) → postoperative bacteremia
  • Septicemia — expansion of bacteremia; serious medical emergency; can escalate to SEPTIC SHOCK / SIRS (Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome)
  • Signs: hyperthermia OR hypothermia, confusion, rash, tachycardia, hypotension
  • ~40% of septicemia patients progress to septic shock; of THOSE, approximately HALF die
  • Highest risk: immunosuppressed/immunocompromised patients
  • Gram-positive AND Gram-negative bacteria EQUALLY responsible; can be community-acquired OR HAI

Infective Endocarditis (IE)

General Definition & Risk Factors
  • Serious infection of the endocardium (innermost heart lining) — covers heart valves
  • Valves suffer valvular incompetence/insufficiency → may lead to intractable CHF + abscesses involving the myocardium
  • Occurs in patients with damaged/abnormal heart architecture + opportunistic bacteria entering vascular system via trauma, infection elsewhere, or high-risk activities (IV drug use, unprotected sex)
  • High morbidity; generally FATAL if untreated
Subacute Native Valve Endocarditis
  • SLOW to develop
  • Usually caused by α-hemolytic Streptococcus (often from oral cavity); also Enterococcus and Staphylococcus
  • From periodontal, pharyngeal, or remote infections
  • Dentists prescribe prophylactic antibiotics before extractions to prevent colonization of pre-existing valve lesions (rheumatic fever, mitral valve stenosis, congenital anomalies)
  • Bacterial colony protected from phagocytosis by blood clots → can break loose → travel to lungs (pulmonary thrombosis) or brain (cerebral infarct)
Acute IE, PVE & IVDA IE
  • Acute IE: SUDDEN onset, involves NORMAL valves, typically S. aureus or Group B Streptococcus — can damage valves rapidly, fatal if untreated
  • PVE (Prosthetic Valve Endocarditis): 5–10% of all IE. Mechanical valves infected EARLY (first 3 months); bioprosthetic valves LATE (after 1 year). Mitral > aortic. S. aureus = most common cause both early and late. Pacemakers/ICDs have similar risk
  • IVDA IE: up to 75% involve NORMAL native valves; 50% involve TRICUSPID valve. FUNGAL IE (Candida albicans) in IV drug users/ICU patients on broad-spectrum antibiotics — blood cultures often NEGATIVE, diagnosis via large emboli microscopy

Clinical Signs of Infective Endocarditis

85% of IE patients have a detectable heart murmur on auscultation. Classic signs (in ~half of IE patients):

  • Subungual hemorrhages (under nails)
  • Petechiae of the skin
  • Janeway lesions — non-tender maculae on palms/soles
  • Osler nodes — TENDER subcutaneous nodules on distal finger/toe pads
  • Roth spots — retinal hemorrhages with clear centers (relatively RARE, 5%)

Other signs: embolic stroke, paralysis, hemiparesis, stiff neck, delirium, distended neck veins, CHF on chest X-ray, hepatosplenomegaly, pericardial/pleural friction rub, gallops/rales, cardiac arrhythmia.

Treatment: antibiotics primary; surgery for lysis of adhesions, valve repair/replacement; CHF treatment + supplemental O2; renal damage may require hemodialysis.

Myocarditis, Pericarditis & Vasculitis

Myocarditis

Inflammation of the middle, muscular layer of the heart (myocardium) — caused by a variety of pathogens:

  • Bacterial: Chlamydia, Mycoplasma, Streptococcus, Staphylococcus, Borrelia, Treponema
  • Viral: Coxsackie B, Cytomegalovirus, Epstein-Barr, Hepatitis C, Herpes simplex, HIV, Parvovirus
  • Fungal: Aspergillus, Candida, Coccidioides, Cryptococcus, Histoplasma
  • Symptoms often mild/absent → undetected → may lead to CARDIOMYOPATHY (heart failure)

Myocarditis and cardiomyopathy are the LEADING CAUSES for heart transplantation in the U.S.

Pericarditis

  • Inflammation of the pericardium (outer layer of heart); several forms
  • Often SECONDARY to other bacterial infections (lung abscesses, pneumonia) — most commonly Streptococcus
  • Less commonly VIRAL — called acute non-specific pericarditis
  • Chronic pericarditis — pericardium binds to heart via adhesions → requires SURGICAL LYSIS OF ADHESIONS
  • Extrapulmonary TB pericarditis (Mycobacterium tuberculosis) — calcium/fibrin deposits thicken/constrict pericardial sac → entraps fluid → CARDIAC TAMPONADE (life-threatening)
  • Pericardiocentesis may be required to drain fluid and relieve pressure

Vasculitis (Angiitis)

Inflammation of Blood Vessels
  • Vascular inflammation outcomes: NARROWING of lumen → reduced blood flow; COMPLETE constriction → ischemia/necrosis; or DILATATION → ANEURYSM formation
  • Severity depends on vessel SIZE and NUMBER involved
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa — produces characteristic BLUE PUS (pigment: pyocyanin) which mediates tissue damage
  • Ecthyma gangrenosum — P. aeruginosa's affinity for invading blood vessels; begins as superficial skin lesions (buttocks, perineum, axillary region, extremities) → center turns BLACK (necrosis from destruction of capillaries/arterioles/venules)
  • Mortality rate HIGH among NEUTROPENIC patients
  • Deep wounds, open bone fractures, severe burns → risk of OSTEOMYELITIS caused by P. aeruginosa

Pseudomonas aeruginosa Endocarditis

  • Patients with prosthetic heart valves and IV drug users prone to P. aeruginosa endocarditis
  • IV drug users: typically younger males, otherwise healthy except substance use disorder — infection from drug paraphernalia contaminated with water-borne bacterial cells
  • TRICUSPID valve (between right atrium and ventricle) MORE often involved than other heart valves
  • Severe form may affect LEFT HEART (left-sided endocarditis) — POOR PROGNOSIS

Blood-Brain Barrier & Meningitis

The Blood-Brain Barrier
  • Physiological barrier between systemic blood and brain PARENCHYMA
  • Prevents entrance of ANTIBODIES and large molecules — BUT remains PERMEABLE to water, oxygen, CO2, and non-ionic solutes (alcohol, glucose, anesthetic drugs)
  • Formed by TIGHT JUNCTIONS between choroid plexus, arachnoid epithelial cells, and capillary endothelial cells
  • Clearance of harmful substances once the barrier is PENETRATED is DIFFICULT
  • NO barrier exists between brain and CSF — CSF is in direct contact with brain matter
  • Free movement of inflammatory cells/pathogens within brain extracellular fluid is RESTRICTED because cellular space between neurons is SMALLER than the smallest viruses
CNS Infections — General Principles
  • CNS tissues are remarkably RESISTANT to infection — but NOT fully impervious; if infection occurs, damage can be SEVERE
  • Brain/spinal cord infections (bacteria, viruses, fungi) are relatively UNCOMMON — the CNS has NO intrinsic immune system
  • QUICK diagnosis and treatment are CRITICAL to preventing death or permanent neurologic deficits

Meningitis — Routes of Entry & Pathogens by Age Group

General & Acute Bacterial Meningitis
  • Pathogens reach the meninges via bloodstream, penetrating wound, or surgical procedure
  • Abscesses can SPREAD from adjoining structures (nasal sinuses, periodontium)
  • Non-infectious causes: sarcoidosis, some cancers, cancer drugs, organ rejection prevention drugs — can mimic infectious meningitis symptoms
  • Acute bacterial meningitis — infection of meningeal PIA MATER and ARACHNOID layers; LIFE-THREATENING, requires IMMEDIATE treatment
  • Bacteria are normal residents of nose/upper respiratory/GI tract — infect CNS if immune system depressed
  • May be a COMPLICATION of endocarditis, pneumonia, or otitis media; any penetrating skull injury (including surgery) can allow bacterial entry

Symptoms: headache, stiff neck, sore throat (after respiratory illness), vomiting. Physician tries to lower patient's chin to chest (may be too painful = positive sign). Older children/adults: irritability, confusion, progression from drowsiness → stupor → coma → death.

Haemophilus influenzae Meningitis (Hib)

  • Hib spreads from upper respiratory tract to sinuses/middle ear → otitis media (children at elevated risk)
  • If bacteria invade bloodstream → travel to lymph nodes → invade meninges → meningitis (mechanism of crossing blood-brain barrier NOT fully understood)
  • Infants ≤3 months: protected by MATERNAL ANTIBODIES (infection rare)
  • Between 6 months–1 year: maternal antibodies decline → PEAK INCIDENCE of disease; up to 90% mortality if UNTREATED
  • Enduring deficits after recovery: hearing loss, blindness, chronic seizures, hydrocephalus, developmental problems

Diagnosis, Treatment & Prevention

  • Diagnostic specimens: CSF (spinal tap), blood samples, sputum, nasopharyngeal swabs, conjunctival exudates
  • Blood cultures highly effective for isolating bacteria; species ID critical for OPTIMAL antibiotic selection
  • Hib vaccine = routine childhood immunization. 3 monovalent conjugate Hib vaccines + 3 combination vaccines containing Hib
  • Schedule: series at 2, 4, and 6 months OR 2 and 4 months (depending on vaccine); booster at 12–15 months
  • RESULT: 99% DECREASE in Hib infections in children <5 years since vaccine protocols implemented in the U.S.

Fungal, Parasitic & Viral Meningitis

Fungal Meningitis
  • Cryptococcus — develops SLOWLY vs. acute bacterial; results in CHRONIC meningitis
  • Other fungi: Histoplasma, Blastomyces, Coccidioides (found in soil); Candida (nearly ALWAYS an HAI)
Parasitic Meningitis — PAM
  • Naegleria fowleri (microscopic amoeba) → Primary Amoebic Meningoencephalitis (PAM)
  • Enters through the NOSE while diving/swimming in warm FRESHWATER (lakes, ponds, rivers, underchlorinated pools, warm springs) — OR via contaminated water heater + Neti pot sinus irrigation
  • Drinking contaminated water is NOT a transmission route
  • NOT found in SALTWATER (coastal waters)
Viral Meningitis — MOST COMMON Form

Causes: non-polio enteroviruses (coxsackievirus, enterovirus, echovirus, parechovirus); mumps (Rubulavirus); herpes viruses (HSV, VZV); measles virus; influenza virus; West Nile virus; lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus. CMV and HIV/AIDS may cause CHRONIC infection.

Encephalitis, Brucellosis & Brain Abscess

Encephalitis
  • Inflammation of the brain parenchyma — MORE DANGEROUS than viral meningitis
  • Caused by SAME viruses as meningitis PLUS arboviruses and rabies
  • Herpes simplex encephalitis — MOST COMMON type in U.S.; up to 70% mortality if UNTREATED
  • Paralytic poliomyelitis — targets select MOTOR NEURONS of the CNS
  • Rabies — caused by LYSSAVIRUS; endemic in U.S. wild animals (skunks, foxes, raccoons, bats); characteristic BULLET SHAPE on scanning electron microscopy; transmission by BITE from infected animal

Brucellosis

  • Bacterial infection affecting BOTH cardiovascular system AND CNS
  • Multiple names based on outbreak location or presentation: Malta fever, fever of Crete, Mediterranean fever, undulant fever, gastric remittent fever
  • Acute: flu-like symptoms + weight loss + abdominal/joint/back pain. Most cases RESOLVE with appropriate antibiotics
  • Chronic: similar to tuberculosis — recurrent symptoms for YEARS
  • Most serious complications: infective endocarditis, meningitis, encephalitis

Brain Abscess & Subdural Empyema

Brain Abscess
  • Collection of PUS — usually in FRONTAL or TEMPORAL lobes of cerebrum; may be FREE or ENCAPSULATED; may occur in MULTIPLE areas
  • Typically SECONDARY to another infection elsewhere in the body
  • Primary infections: otitis media, sinusitis, mastoiditis; bacteria can also travel from a tooth abscess or heart/lung infections
  • Symptoms = those typically associated with increased intracranial pressure (ICP)
Subdural Empyema
  • Collection of pus just UNDER the dural meninges
  • Frequently a complication of sinusitis or otitis media; OR from bacteria entering via penetrating head injury
  • Surgery may be required to drain pus and relieve intracranial pressure
OR Clinical Connection — Discussion Activity: A craniotomy for excision of a left-sided intracranial lesion reveals an ENCAPSULATED MASS filled with PURULENT MATERIAL — consistent with a BRAIN ABSCESS. The patient's history of a recent BLOOD TRANSFUSION for anemia is highly relevant — bacterial pathogens (S. aureus, S. epidermidis) from blood transfusion contamination can seed distant sites including the brain, presenting later as an abscess. Other circulatory-system-spread infections to consider: infective endocarditis with septic emboli to the brain (cerebral infarct → secondary abscess), and bacteremia/septicemia from any source crossing the blood-brain barrier.

Putting It All Together — Why CV and CNS Infections Are Linked

Pathogens readily move from the cardiovascular system to the CNS because: (1) the brain receives a massive blood supply and pathogens circulating in blood are delivered directly to brain capillaries; (2) the blood-brain barrier, while protective, can be PENETRATED by certain organisms or during inflammation/injury, after which clearance is DIFFICULT; (3) once past the barrier, there is NO additional barrier between blood vessels/CSF and brain parenchyma; (4) septic emboli from endocarditis can lodge in cerebral vessels causing infarcts that become secondary abscesses; (5) the CNS has NO intrinsic immune system to mount a rapid local defense.

Structural abnormalities present from birth (congenital heart anomalies) that can create pre-existing lesions on heart valves, predisposing patients to subacute infective endocarditis.

The act of listening to internal body sounds (typically with a stethoscope), such as heart murmurs, gallops, rales, or friction rubs — used to detect signs of infective endocarditis, pericarditis, or CHF.
Bioprosthetic Valve
Click to reveal
A heart valve replacement made from biological tissue (often porcine or bovine). More likely than mechanical valves to become infected LATE (after 1 year) in prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE).
Cardiac Arrhythmia
Click to reveal
An irregular heartbeat — can be an observed sign of infective endocarditis due to damage to the heart's conduction system or muscle from infection/inflammation.
Cardiac Tamponade
Click to reveal
A life-threatening condition where fluid accumulates in the pericardial sac, compressing the heart and preventing it from filling/pumping properly. Can result from extrapulmonary TB pericarditis. Treated with pericardiocentesis.
Cardiomyopathy
Click to reveal
A disease of the heart muscle that can result from untreated/undetected myocarditis. Myocarditis and cardiomyopathy together are the leading causes of heart transplantation in the U.S.
Cardioverter Defibrillator
Click to reveal
An implanted device that monitors heart rhythm and delivers electrical shocks to correct dangerous arrhythmias. Carries a similar infection risk profile to prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE).
Cerebral Infarct
Click to reveal
Death of brain tissue due to interrupted blood supply (stroke) — can result when an embolus from an infected heart valve (infective endocarditis) breaks loose and travels to the brain.
Choroid Plexus
Click to reveal
A network of cells in the brain's ventricles that produces cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Tight junctions between choroid plexus cells, arachnoid epithelial cells, and capillary endothelial cells form the blood-brain barrier.
Electrophoresis
Click to reveal
A laboratory technique that separates proteins (or other molecules) based on size/charge using an electric field. Used in the Western blot test to separate serum proteins before transferring them to a filter for antibody detection.
Emboli
Click to reveal
Detached fragments (e.g., blood clots containing bacterial colonies) that travel through the bloodstream and can lodge in distant vessels, causing pulmonary thrombosis, cerebral infarct, or other organ damage — a complication of infective endocarditis.
Empyema (subdural)
Click to reveal
A collection of pus just under the dural meninges of the brain — frequently a complication of sinusitis or otitis media, or from penetrating head injury. May require surgical drainage to relieve intracranial pressure.
Extrapulmonary (TB)
Click to reveal
Tuberculosis infection occurring OUTSIDE the lungs — e.g., extrapulmonary TB pericarditis, where M. tuberculosis causes calcium/fibrin deposits that thicken and constrict the pericardial sac, leading to cardiac tamponade.
Gallops
Click to reveal
Abnormal extra heart sounds (S3 or S4) heard on auscultation, often associated with heart failure — can be an observed sign in patients with infective endocarditis or myocarditis-related cardiomyopathy.
Heart Murmur
Click to reveal
An abnormal whooshing/swishing sound heard during the cardiac cycle, caused by turbulent blood flow — often from damaged/incompetent valves. Present in 85% of infective endocarditis patients on auscultation.
Hemodialysis
Click to reveal
A medical procedure that filters waste/excess fluid from the blood when the kidneys cannot. May be required for patients with infective endocarditis who develop renal damage.
Hepatosplenomegaly
Click to reveal
Enlargement of both the liver and spleen — an observed clinical sign that can occur in infective endocarditis and other systemic infections (also seen in amebiasis, Epstein-Barr virus infection).
Hypotension
Click to reveal
Abnormally low blood pressure — a sign of septicemia/septic shock, reflecting cardiovascular collapse from systemic infection and inflammatory response.
Hypothermia
Click to reveal
Abnormally low body temperature — one of the possible temperature-related signs of septicemia (the other being hyperthermia/fever); reflects dysregulated systemic inflammatory response.
Intractable CHF
Click to reveal
Congestive heart failure that is difficult or impossible to control with standard treatment — can result from valvular incompetence caused by infective endocarditis damaging heart valves.
Ischemia
Click to reveal
Insufficient blood supply to a tissue, causing oxygen/nutrient deprivation — can result from vasculitis causing complete constriction of blood vessel lumen, potentially progressing to necrosis.
Janeway Lesions
Click to reveal
Non-tender, flat (macular) red/purple spots on the palms of the hands and soles of the feet — a classic sign of infective endocarditis, distinguished from Osler nodes by being non-tender.
Lysis of Adhesions
Click to reveal
A surgical procedure to cut/separate fibrous bands (adhesions) that have formed between tissues — required in chronic pericarditis when the pericardium binds to the heart, and in some cases of infective endocarditis.
Morbidity
Click to reveal
The state of being diseased, or the rate of disease in a population. Infective endocarditis is described as having high morbidity and generally being fatal if left untreated.
Neti Pot
Click to reveal
A device used for nasal/sinus irrigation. If filled with contaminated (non-sterile/non-distilled) water, can introduce Naegleria fowleri into the sinuses, where it can travel to the brain and cause primary amoebic meningoencephalitis (PAM).
Osler Nodes
Click to reveal
TENDER, raised subcutaneous nodules, usually found on the distal pads of the fingers/toes — a classic sign of infective endocarditis; distinguished from Janeway lesions by being painful/tender.
Paraphernalia (drug)
Click to reveal
Equipment used for illicit drug use (needles, syringes, etc.) — when contaminated with water-borne bacteria (e.g., Pseudomonas aeruginosa), can be a source of infective endocarditis in IV drug users.
Parenchyma
Click to reveal
The functional tissue of an organ, as distinguished from its supporting/connective framework. "Brain parenchyma" refers to the functional brain tissue that the blood-brain barrier protects, and that becomes inflamed in encephalitis.
Pericardiocentesis
Click to reveal
A procedure to drain fluid from the pericardial sac using a needle/catheter — used to relieve cardiac tamponade caused by conditions like extrapulmonary TB pericarditis.
Rales
Click to reveal
Abnormal crackling/bubbling lung sounds heard on auscultation, often indicating fluid in the airways — can be an observed sign in infective endocarditis patients with associated congestive heart failure.
Roth Spots
Click to reveal
Retinal hemorrhages with pale/clear centers, seen on fundoscopic exam — a relatively rare (5%) but classic sign of infective endocarditis.
Sarcoidosis
Click to reveal
A non-infectious inflammatory disease that can cause granuloma formation in various organs, including the meninges — can produce symptoms similar to infectious meningitis without an infectious cause.
Stenosis
Click to reveal
Abnormal narrowing of a passage or opening — e.g., mitral valve stenosis is a pre-existing heart valve condition that can predispose patients to subacute infective endocarditis.
Subungual
Click to reveal
Located beneath the fingernail or toenail. Subungual hemorrhages (small areas of bleeding under the nails, sometimes called "splinter hemorrhages") are a classic sign of infective endocarditis.
Tachycardia
Click to reveal
Abnormally rapid heart rate — one of the signs of septicemia, reflecting the body's compensatory response to systemic infection and the inflammatory cascade.
Transfusions
Click to reveal
The administration of donated blood or blood products into a patient's circulation. Usually safe and life-saving, but can rarely transmit bacterial, viral, parasitic, or prion pathogens despite donor screening protocols.

Chapter 17 Practice Quiz

Select the best answer, then submit for your score.

1. A patient with septicemia progresses to septic shock. According to chapter statistics, approximately what percentage of septicemia patients progress to septic shock, and what is the approximate mortality once septic shock develops?

✓ Correct! Nearly 40% of patients with septicemia progress to septic shock (SIRS), and of those patients, approximately half will die from the infection. This underscores why septicemia is considered a serious medical emergency requiring rapid recognition and treatment.
✗ Incorrect. Nearly 40% of septicemia patients progress to septic shock, and approximately HALF of those patients die from the infection — making septicemia a serious medical emergency.

2. A patient with a damaged mitral valve from prior rheumatic fever develops a slow-developing infection of the endocardium following a dental extraction. Which organism is the MOST LIKELY cause, and what type of IE is this?

✓ Correct! Subacute native valve endocarditis is SLOW to develop and usually caused by α-hemolytic Streptococcus, often originating from the oral cavity (as in this dental extraction scenario). Pre-existing valve lesions from rheumatic fever provide a site for bacterial colonization. This is exactly why dentists prescribe prophylactic antibiotics before extractions in at-risk patients.
✗ Incorrect. The combination of a SLOW-developing infection, a PRE-EXISTING valve lesion (from rheumatic fever), and a DENTAL procedure points to subacute native valve endocarditis caused by α-hemolytic Streptococcus from the oral cavity. S. aureus causes ACUTE IE on normal valves. P. aeruginosa is associated with IVDA. Candida causes fungal IE typically in IV drug users/ICU patients.

3. Which pair of clinical signs of infective endocarditis is correctly distinguished by tenderness?

✓ Correct! Janeway lesions are NON-TENDER, flat (macular) lesions on the palms and soles. Osler nodes are TENDER, raised subcutaneous nodules typically on the distal pads of the fingers and toes. This tenderness distinction is the classic way to differentiate the two on physical exam — both are signs of infective endocarditis.
✗ Incorrect. The key distinction: Janeway lesions = NON-TENDER maculae (flat spots) on palms/soles. Osler nodes = TENDER nodules on distal finger/toe pads. Roth spots are retinal hemorrhages (not a skin finding with tenderness). Subungual hemorrhages and petechiae are not classically distinguished by tenderness in this chapter.

4. Myocarditis often goes undetected due to mild or absent symptoms, but can lead to which serious long-term condition that is a leading cause of heart transplantation in the U.S.?

✓ Correct! Myocarditis (inflammation of the myocardium) can progress to cardiomyopathy — a disease of the heart muscle resulting in heart failure. Myocarditis and cardiomyopathy together are the LEADING CAUSES of heart transplantation in the United States. Because myocarditis symptoms are often mild or absent, the condition frequently goes undetected until significant cardiac damage has occurred.
✗ Incorrect. Myocarditis can progress to CARDIOMYOPATHY (heart muscle disease/failure), which along with myocarditis itself is a leading cause of heart transplantation in the U.S. Cardiac tamponade results from pericardial fluid accumulation (e.g., from TB pericarditis), not myocarditis progression.

5. A patient with extrapulmonary tuberculosis develops calcium and fibrin deposits that thicken and constrict the pericardial sac, trapping fluid and preventing the heart from moving freely. What is this life-threatening condition, and what procedure relieves it?

✓ Correct! This describes cardiac tamponade — a life-threatening condition where fluid is trapped in the pericardial sac (here, due to TB-related calcium/fibrin deposits constricting the sac), compressing the heart and preventing normal filling/movement. Pericardiocentesis (needle/catheter drainage of the pericardial fluid) is used to relieve the pressure.
✗ Incorrect. This scenario describes cardiac tamponade — fluid trapped in a constricted pericardial sac compressing the heart, caused here by extrapulmonary TB pericarditis. Pericardiocentesis (fluid drainage) relieves the pressure. Lysis of adhesions is used for chronic pericarditis adhesions but doesn't address acute tamponade fluid accumulation.

6. A neutropenic patient develops superficial skin lesions on the buttocks and perineum that grow and turn black in the center. Blue-tinged pus is noted. Which organism and syndrome best explain this presentation?

✓ Correct! Pseudomonas aeruginosa produces a characteristic BLUE PUS due to the pigment pyocyanin. Its affinity for invading blood vessels causes ecthyma gangrenosum — superficial skin lesions (often on buttocks, perineum, axillary region, extremities) that grow and develop a BLACK center from necrosis (destruction of capillaries, arterioles, and venules). Mortality is high among neutropenic patients — exactly the population described.
✗ Incorrect. The combination of BLUE pus (pyocyanin pigment) and black-centered, growing skin lesions in a neutropenic patient is classic for Pseudomonas aeruginosa causing ecthyma gangrenosum — a vasculitis-related syndrome with high mortality in neutropenic patients. S. aureus, Naegleria, and M. tuberculosis do not produce this specific presentation.

7. Which statement about the blood-brain barrier is accurate?

It is formed by tight junctions between the choroid plexus, arachnoid epithelial cells, and capillary endothelial cells. 8. An infant between 6 months and 1 year of age is at PEAK risk for Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) meningitis. What is the primary reason for this age-specific vulnerability?

✓ Correct! Infants 3 months and younger are protected by maternal antibodies, making infection rare in this age group. Between 6 months and 1 year, these maternal antibodies have declined significantly, creating a window of vulnerability before the infant's own immune system (often supported by the Hib vaccine series starting at 2 months) provides full protection. This corresponds with the peak incidence of Hib disease, which carries up to 90% mortality if untreated.
✗ Incorrect. The peak incidence between 6 months and 1 year occurs because maternal antibodies (protective for infants ≤3 months) have declined by this age, while the infant's own immunity (including from the Hib vaccine series) may not yet be fully established. The blood-brain barrier exists from early development; this is not about skull injuries or age-restricted transmissibility.

9. A patient develops meningitis-like symptoms after swimming and diving in a warm freshwater lake, with the organism gaining entry through the nose. Which organism and condition does this describe, and which statement about transmission is TRUE?

✓ Correct! Naegleria fowleri is a microscopic amoeba causing primary amoebic meningoencephalitis (PAM). It enters through the NOSE during diving/swimming in warm freshwater (lakes, ponds, rivers, underchlorinated pools, warm springs) — or via contaminated Neti pot water. Importantly, DRINKING contaminated water is NOT a transmission route, and N. fowleri is NOT found in saltwater/coastal waters.
✗ Incorrect. The scenario (warm freshwater, nasal entry during swimming/diving) describes Naegleria fowleri causing primary amoebic meningoencephalitis (PAM). Key facts: drinking contaminated water does NOT transmit PAM, and the organism is NOT found in saltwater. Cryptococcus causes chronic fungal meningitis but is not freshwater/nasal-entry associated in this way.

10. A craniotomy reveals an encapsulated mass filled with purulent material in the frontal lobe. The patient's history includes a recent blood transfusion for anemia. Which condition was most likely found, and what is the clinical significance of the transfusion history?

✓ Correct! An encapsulated mass filled with purulent material in the frontal lobe describes a BRAIN ABSCESS. The recent blood transfusion is clinically significant: transfusion-transmitted bacterial contaminants (such as S. aureus or S. epidermidis, introduced via percutaneous needle puncture during collection/administration) can cause bacteremia/septicemia, with hematogenous spread allowing bacteria to cross into the CNS and form a secondary abscess — connecting the "Transmission of Infections from Donated Blood Products" content directly to a CNS finding.
✗ Incorrect. An encapsulated, pus-filled mass in the frontal lobe describes a BRAIN ABSCESS, not tamponade, empyema, or PAM. The blood transfusion history is highly relevant: bacterial contaminants from transfusion (S. aureus, S. epidermidis) can cause bloodstream infection that seeds the brain, forming an abscess — illustrating how circulatory system infections can lead to CNS findings. Naegleria fowleri is freshwater/nasal-transmitted, not transfusion-related.
